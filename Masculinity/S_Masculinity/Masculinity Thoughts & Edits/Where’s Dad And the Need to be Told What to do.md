Today the self-help industry is worth $15 billion. It includes self-help books, apps, and coaching services. Over the next ten years expert forecasts a nearly 8% year-over-year growth rate for the industry.

I write a lot about self-improvement, but I really do it for myself. I’m fascinated with how pseudo psychological research worms its way into childhood development through curriculum and social media. It takes almost no time to read often mention studies or to find out that actual psychologists no longer believe the very things we’re being taught.

As information starts to disseminate, listeners stop fact-checking and start taking it as truth. After all, if you’re hearing it from your favorite influencer or on the news you have to assume that they’ve done that work for you.

But, here’s the rub, does anyone ever go back and fact check? Does anyone ever go back and see if the claims they made in the past were true?

No. Why would they?

This creates a huge problem in the self-help world. There ends up being a massive disconnect between what is fact and what people believe. Hardly any psychological research ends up being fact. Psychology as a whole is grappling with decades of falsified research reports. It’s toppled many famous studies and researchers, or at least set their work back decades.
